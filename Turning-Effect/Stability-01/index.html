Stability & Centre of Gravity

Understand the science of balancing and what makes objects stable.

What Determines Stability?

An object's stability describes how difficult it is to topple. It depends on two primary design factors:

  • Height of the Centre of Gravity (CG): The lower the CG, the more stable the object.
  • Width of the Base: The wider the base, the more stable the object.
A slight tilt causes the tall thin box to topple, while the wide low box returns upright.

The Tipping Rule

When an object is tilted, its base is lifted off the ground, pivoting about its bottom edge.

The Rule:
  • If the vertical line from the CG falls within the base, gravity creates a restoring moment that returns it to upright.
  • If the vertical line falls outside the base, gravity creates a toppling moment that causes it to fall.
The critical tipping point occurs when the vertical line from CG is exactly on the edge pivot.

Three Types of Equilibrium

When an object is in equilibrium, it can react to a displacement in three distinct ways:

Stable

Returns to its original position after a slight displacement.

Unstable

Topples and moves away from its original position after a slight displacement.

Neutral

Remains in its new position when displaced. Its CG height does not change.
