Concept Introduction

Understand key hazards and essential safety features in home electricity.

The Three Electrical Hazards

⚡

Damaged Insulation

When insulating plastic wears off or cracks, exposing conducting copper wires. Anyone touching it gets an electric shock.

🔥

Overheating of Cables

Occurs when drawing too much current (overloaded socket) or using thin wires. Can lead to fire.

💧

Damp Conditions

Water lowers skin electrical resistance. Touching switches/appliances with wet hands dramatically increases current flow through the body.

Safety Devices

🩺

Fuse & Fuse Rating

Contains a thin wire that melts and breaks the circuit when the current exceeds its rating. This prevents the circuit wires from getting extremely hot and catching fire.

Standard Ratings:
1 A 3 A 5 A 13 A
🔄

Circuit Breakers

An electromagnetic switch that trips and cuts off current when it is too high. Unlike fuses, they do not melt and can be reset after fixing the fault.

Shock Prevention

🔌

Earthing Metal Casings

If a fault causes the live wire to touch the metal casing, a massive current flows through the earth wire to ground instead of shocking a user. This large current melts the fuse, cutting the supply.

🔳

Double Insulation

Appliances with plastic casings (non-conducting bodies) have two layers of insulation. Since the body cannot conduct electricity, an earth wire is not needed.

Mains Wiring Functions

Live (L)
Brown: Delivers current from the source to the appliance (at high voltage, usually 230V). Switches, fuses, and circuit breakers must be connected to this wire.
Neutral (N)
Blue: Completes the circuit by returning current back to the source (at or near 0V).
Earth (E)
Yellow & Green stripes: A safety wire connected to the metal body. Path of low resistance to route dangerous currents safely into the ground.

Step-by-Step Working

Formula: \[I = \frac{P}{V}\]

Step 1: Identify Given Values

  • Power, \(P = 1000\text{ W}\)
  • Voltage, \(V = 240\text{ V}\)

Step 2: Calculate Current (\(I\))

\(I = \frac{1000\text{ W}}{240\text{ V}} = 4.17\text{ A}\)

Step 3: Select Fuse Rating

The fuse rating must be the **smallest standard rating** that is **greater than** the calculated current of 4.17 A.

Standard Fuse Ratings: 1 A, 3 A, 5 A, 13 A.

Since 4.17 A is greater than 3 A but less than 5 A, we select the 5 A fuse.
